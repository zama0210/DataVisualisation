Data Visualization

A Close Reading - Overview of Data Visualisation

Welcome to the third instalment of the blog series. In this post, we will critically reflect on the reading, “What is the Interaction for Data Visualisation” by Evanthia Dimara and Charles Périn.

The reading provides an overall analysis and demonstration of interactivity within data visualisation. The article follows a set of data collected by the authors about different articles made by other authors whilst they made observations regarding Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) as well as providing readers insight into the topic at hand, interaction for data visualisation.

The article highlights the importance of interaction in data visualisation and the clear need for a definition of the term. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the different perspectives on interaction in the visualisation community and HCI, which helps clarify the concept. One of the most notable things discussed under the frame of interaction was the importance of human-driven interaction with data, by the actions performed after seeing data.

While reading this article, the following section provided a foundation for understanding how good interaction is made.(a good majority of these will be quoted directly from the article):

These concepts specify what formulates a good interaction. Dialogue, the first concept, is the most prominent from the early history of HCI. This concept looks at interaction as a cycle of communication (Dimara and Périn, 2020). “Interaction is channelled through input and output from a computer perspective, and through perception and action from a user perspective”. This quote provides a contextualised summary of what this concept entails.

Transmission sees interaction as a transference of “information between the computer and the user”. As a rule of thumb for this concept, good interaction maximises the transmission rate. This is determined is through using the information theory which states that “the transmission rate is the amount of error-free information per time unit that is transferred over a communication channel in the presence of noise” (Dimara and Périn, 2020).

Control is the third concept that sees interaction as a minimization of error concerning some user target. The control theory drives this concept. The theory states that “interaction is continuous and defined through a system that consists of goals, inputs, outputs, feedback, feedforward and states” (Dimara and Périn, 2020).

Tool Use sees interaction “as a series of tools that allow a user to interact with a computer”. According to the activity theory and the instrumental interaction model, the tool is a mediator between an object of interest and the user(Dimara and Périn, 2020).

Optimal Behaviour utilises interaction as an adaptive behaviour under user capabilities, task, interface, and environmental constraints. “It is based on the rationality paradigm and adaptation theories such as information foraging that state that humans tend to approximate optimal adaptation of their motor, perceptual and cognitive processes” (Dimara and Périn, 2020).

Embodiment sees interaction as the act of being and participating in the world, in which the users feel that the technology is an extension of them(Dimara and Périn, 2020).

Experience, as the name suggests, sees interaction as a phenomenon shaped by user expectations, momentary reactions, and recall. It places human experience as a key factor in how interaction unfolds(Dimara and Périn, 2020).

By integrating these concepts, the authors state that good interaction comes from maximising these concepts and applying the theories that accompany them to the interaction.

The authors provide extensive examples of what they mean by the importance of human responses to the data by answering the question, "Is this an interaction for visualisation?” I will provide a few quotes that demonstrate the previous statement. Before providing these examples, they give a compact definition of interaction in this context. “Interaction for visualization is the interplay between a person and a data interface involving a data-related intent, at least one action from the person and an interface reaction that is perceived as such” (Dimara and Périn, 2020).

The authors create a definition of interaction based on two assumptions: i)interactions with visualisations are a subset of interactions with interactive systems, and ii) interactions with interactive systems are a subset of interactions with people’s minds and environments.

Following this definition, here are the examples provided by the authors :

“SCENARIO 1: Joe is staring at a visualization without performing any action that is interpretable by the visualization. He is reasoning (mentally) about the visualized data. There are further (computer-initiated) animations and real-time changes that show important information that Joe considers in his reasoning process.

Answer: No. While there were several actions from the person, the visualization did not return any reaction as a response to those actions.

SCENARIO 2: Joe wants to focus on a cluster of data points. Using a mouse, he traces a lasso to select the data points. The visualization draws the lasso with the same colour as the background.

Answer: No. While there was an action reaction, the reaction of the system was not perceived by the person.

SCENARIO 3: Joe notices a slider widget. He intends to filter out some data points. He moves the slider, and the visualization is zoomed in. He does not understand what is going on.

Answer: Yes. Because the reaction of the visualization (although not understood) was perceived by the person.

SCENARIO 4: An airport provides a visualization that updates automatically to show all departure gate data. Jack looks for his gate number, but the visualization changes too quickly. A gaze detector tracks Jack’s unfocused gaze attempts and slows down the rate at which the visualization changes. Jack now locates his gate number.

Answer: Yes. There was an action-reaction perceived by the person. Such gazing action was not interpretable by the interface in scenario 1.

SCENARIO 5: Averell cannot remember where he parked his car. In the parking, a screen shows car CO2 emission data to motivate car drivers to use their cars less. Averell is not careful, and he bumps his head on the screen. Because the screen is touch-enabled, this contact rearranges the data points. Averell sees some graphics moving around but does not pay attention. He ignores the screen and continues his search.

Answer: No. While there was an action-reaction perceived by the person, there was no data-related intent (unless the designer, having such intent, had caused Averell to non-consciously process some data).

SCENARIO 6: Like Averell, William bumps his head on the display. But when he sees the graphics move around, he gets curious and starts touching the display to explore what these graphics things are about.

Answer: Yes. Although the interaction was not initiated by an explicit intent, there was an implicit data-related intent involved in the process” (Dimara and Périn, 2020).

These scenarios helped me engage with the arguments the authors had at hand and I relied on these scenarios as a source of understanding of what interaction is and its role in data visualisation.

I believe that the authors would have missed the mark had they excluded these scenarios as well as the basic phrasing of their research, their formulas, how they conducted their research, how it was linked to interaction and how their research aided in providing a broader explanation to the question they are asking in the title.

By separating the information into well-organised sections, the authors broke down their explanations and examples without compromising the necessary jargon or excluding important points to appeal to the general audience.

References

  1. Dimara, E. and Perin, C. (2020). What is Interaction for Data Visualization? IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics, 26(1), pp.119–129. doi:https://doi.org/10.1109/tvcg.2019.293283.
