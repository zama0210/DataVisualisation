Data Visualization

A Critical Reflection of my proposed UI and UX

In this blog post I will be taking the opportunity to critically reflect on the UI and UX design of my website using resources to justify the decisions that I made in the construction of the website.

Before implementing any UI and UX principles I decided to search for examples of data visualization website so that I could take note of what was common amongst these websites. I had to consider that my website would have additional webpages and that it would have a greater focus on my academics. I started with a basic idea of what would be included in my navigation bar before starting with a few low-fidelity wireframes on a piece of paper.

At this point, I was focused on the interface because I wanted to give myself an opportunity to do a thorough UX analysis.

Initially, I wanted the hero section to have a background image and the main colour for this image would be white so that when the user scrolls there’s no real change in the background, the change would happen with the body, with the text. Through trial and error, I decided that it would be better if I left the image out of the hero section. I chose to do the DONKI API for this assignment with the focus being on Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs) so I wanted the Hero section to give a glimpse of what the data presented will be about. All images I could find of the related topic made the hero section jarring to open and it minimised the aesthetic I was trying to build. The images were screaming at you and the information in that section became hard to read.

My overview section was intended to provide the user a SparkNotes version of what CMEs are and how they are measured so when the user navigates to the visualisation page, they would have a basic understanding what they will be looking at. An overview section was what was missing from the example website I chose to look at. At various points, I would click the link to the website, and I would sit in confusion to what I was looking at and how this data had been collected.

I wanted the provided information to be in an asymmetrical balance with an accompanying picture and I intended for a large separation between paragraphs so that it would be easier to digest. This section of the homepage is where I believe I failed at maintaining a clean and simple design. The end product was cluttered.

The blog needed to look different to the rest of the website because I wanted there to be a separation between it and the rest of the site. Blogs are websites so incorporating it as a webpage instead of its own website, took away the simplicity I was looking for. I considered reusing the structure from the previous submission, but I decided against it and used “A crash course on User Experience + Interface Design” pdf to jog my memory and I kept Fitt’s law at the forefront of my mind when I was designing how the cards would look. I decided against have a “Read More” button on the card as it created clutter in the previous submission, and I wanted to avoid it. I wanted to make sure that the actual posts were legible. I wanted to have an edged alignment for the posts, but I did not want the text to create a busy aesthetic. I also settled on having the references next to the post as opposed to the end of the post, but I realise now that it makes the post harder to reader and it takes away from my initial intentions.

In my style guide, I ensured to put the colour scheme and the chosen typography for the hierarchical headings as well as the paragraphs. I opted out of implementing a grid system (though I understood that it would maintain a consistent layout) because the content on each webpage would be vastly different. I tried my best to ensure consistency with the above-mentioned categories instead of a layout because I did not think it would fit well, considering the requirements for the website. My previous assignment submission was used as a point of reflection and as a way to see how I could strengthen the weaknesses I had at that time.

Moving forward, I plan on improving the interface of the blog, ensuring that all my images display even after I have submitted the assignment and that I make some tweaks with the responsive interface because I viewed my website on my phone, and I have grown to dislike how the interface looks there in comparison to on my laptop.

Reflecting on the design decisions I have made has been an enlightening process. I have used the provided UX and UI guide as the main assistants in the construction of this website and I will continue to refer to these guides to ensure I do not reflect again and find myself with the same grievances as I have had before. I am confident that the end-product will be exceptional.

ZamahayiseHlatshwayo
