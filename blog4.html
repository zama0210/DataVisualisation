Data Visualization

A Critical Reflection of My Data-Driven Art

In this blog post, I will reflect on implementing a data-driven artwork for my website. This reflection will critically assess the representation and communication strategies I employed in the artwork.

Before defining any objectives, I had to start with understanding what a data-driven artwork entails. I began by simply googling the definition of it. This definition would help me with understanding how I would approach the artwork and it would be at the forefront of the journey to understanding. That difficulty posed a challenge for how I would plan the artwork. I was not receiving anything useful or what I was looking for was said in a roundabout way (there was a struggle to form a clear understanding of what data-driven artwork is).

Thankfully, the consultation with a tutor prompted me to a broader understanding of what was required of me. That is when I started thinking about what message I wanted to convey concerning the CME API.

Together with the examples I was provided in the consultation, I re-analysed the data the API presented, and I picked the dataset that I found to be useful for the artwork. I wanted to create an artwork with a map of the world. This map would be made up of pulsating circles that form the countries of the world. The data would show where the CMEs have happened. Early into the development of this map, I realised that unless I had an abundance of data from this API, which I realised that it would become increasingly hard to implement the artwork as I wanted seeing as my static data was not rendering the abundance of data that I needed it to.

I started approaching the artwork differently, completely abandoning the whole map approach. I still wanted the objective to remain the same, but I thought of creating an image of an actual Coronal Mass Ejection with the data in the circles. I started by looking at images of the coronal mass ejections and ensuring that they were usable at the very least.

The identification of the data not being enough for the artwork severely worried me and it made me doubt and delay the implementation of this second idea. I liked the idea because I had only two pictures throughout my website of the Coronal Mass Ejections and I thought that even if I were to be unable to create a map of CMEs, I at least wanted my viewers to gain a better perspective of CMEs whilst still offering information about CMEs.

I also wanted to make the jargon associated with CMEs broken down, as well as have the dataset be explained creatively to ensure the user engagement remains the same throughout the website.

However, I was struggling to push for a new perspective on this artwork, so I referred to the reading “Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics” by Fernanda B. Viégas and Martin Wattenberg. I was not looking for anything specific, but I thought that the reading would be a great tool to help with brainstorming an artwork appropriate for the objectives I had laid out.

It became clear that I needed to recalibrate and redefine what I thought of the chosen API. “A second point is that our definition avoids the issue of beauty: we do not contend that beautiful scientific visualizations are automatically artistic, or that visualization art must be pretty” (Viégas and Wattenberg, 2007). This statement was a godsend because I realised that I was trying to make it aesthetically pleasing first before making it functional. It became evident when I returned to the plans, I had for the data art and the pictures I downloaded as examples.

I started over this time with a fresher mind. I looked at the ideas I had before, and I started critiquing them. I asked myself questions like, “Why did I think it would work with the CME API?”, “What did I like about the idea?”, “Why did I like the idea” and “How could I have made it better?”. Answering those questions and identifying what was common among these ideas.

I realised that I preferred to show the data that I wanted to display without physically telling the user what the artwork was displaying (showing rather than telling) but I wanted to create something fun and creative without severely affecting how the art would be rendered by the data.

Overall, I believe I tried my best to implement the data art and to convey the data I had been working with throughout the semester in the best way possible by considering various factors that could jeopardise meeting the assigned requirements as well as maintaining a consistent aesthetic.

References

  1. Viégas, F.B. and Wattenberg, M. (2007). Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics. Lecture Notes in Computer Science, [online] pp.182–191.doi: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-540-73257-0_21.
