Data Visualization

Data Visualisation, Art, and Representation

When it comes to representing data in visual forms, two popular methods often come to mind: data visualization and data art. While they may seem similar at first glance, these two techniques have unique purposes and approaches to representation. In this essay, we will delve into the readings to examine the relationship between data visualization, data art, and their strategies for representing information.

Data visualization is a presentation of data in a graphical or visual format. A data visualization tool is responsible for the visual representations of this data, an example of this is Tableau or D3.js. It offers an intuitive way of analysing data interactively, in turn, it helps users identify patterns, hypothesize correlations and causations, and offers support to the act of sense-making.

Data visualization also offers the opportunity to understand data, as well as draw conclusions at first glance. Some examples of data visualization include bar graphs, pie charts, scatter plots and line graphs.

Data art, however, is not just about presenting data in a visually appealing manner. It goes beyond that and aims to captivate and engage the audience. Breaking away from traditional methods of data representation encourages viewers to think critically and engage with the data in a more meaningful way. Some notable examples of data visualization in the art and design world include Dear Data, a project that involved exchanging postcards with hand-drawn data visualizations depicting personal experiences, and Wind Map, an animated visualization of wind patterns in the US using lines and curves. These projects showcase the potential of data art to evoke emotions and spark curiosity in its viewers.

Both data visualisation and data art offer complexity when it comes to the representation of data. It is important to identify the strategies of representation to be able to discuss the relationship between representation, data art and data visualization.

Data visualization and art differ in their strategies of representation in two ways:

  1. The main goals and objectives of data visualization and data art diverge in their approach. While data visualization strives to simplify and clarify complex data, its main purpose is to convey important insights and conclusions from data analysis to assist in decision-making. It is all about presenting information in a visually appealing and comprehensible way, weaving together numbers and figures to tell a compelling story and captivate the audience. On the other hand, data art serves a different purpose altogether - one of artistic expression rather than practical utility. Its main aim is to entertain and evoke an aesthetic experience, utilizing intricate arrangements of shapes and colours to engage the audience and evoke powerful emotions.

  2. Data art is a unique form of artistic expression that defies traditional norms and pushes boundaries. Its purpose is to transform raw data into captivating visual representations, utilizing specialized tools and software. By doing so, it aims to evoke emotion and imagination in its audience. Unlike traditional data visualization, which adheres to strict guidelines, data art takes a more creative and unconventional approach. It utilizes basic visual elements like lines, shapes, colours, and quantity, and incorporates Gestalt principles to tell compelling stories through simplicity, proximity, similarity, continuity, and closure. Through this, data art delivers meaningful insights and findings in a visually stunning and thought-provoking manner

"Artistic Data Visualisation: Beyond Visual Analytics" by Fernanda B. Viégas and Martin Wattenberg (2007) delves into the fascinating world of artistic visualization. Through their thought-provoking insights, they shed light on how data can be transformed into a beautiful and meaningful form of art. As they explore the concept, the authors highlight the artist's intention to use data as a medium for their artistic expression, and the complex nature of defining both artistic visualization and art.

When it comes to artistic representation and data art, the techniques used share many similarities. Both rely on visual aids such as charts, graphs, and maps to convey information. However, data art takes things a step further by using data visualization as a means of creating art. This unique approach offers a fresh perspective on the endless possibilities of expressing creativity through visualizing data. As previously mentioned, it truly blurs the lines between art and data.

In her essay "How Art Can Contribute to Ethical Reflection on Risky Technologies" (2016), Sabine Roeser delves into the central idea that art has the power to spark ethical reflection when it explores and interacts with risky technologies. By evoking emotions, translating abstract issues into tangible forms, delving into uncharted possibilities, pushing the boundaries of our imagination, and expanding our narrow personal views, art can catalyze ethical reflection. This idea not only applies to the realm of art focused on risky technologies but can also be applied to data art and data visualization in a more general sense. As mentioned in the respective definitions, the objective is to elicit engagement and this is usually done by evoking emotions, and hypotheses, concluding and enabling the viewers to critically think about the data that is presented to them.

In his work, Stuart Hall (1997) outlines the constructionist approach to representation, which encompasses two key methods: the semiotic approach and the discourse approach. According to Hall, representation is a crucial aspect of culture formation, as it enables us to describe and imagine the world around us through language and meaning. This concept can be likened to the idea of encoding and decoding, where encoding refers to the packaging of information (in this case, data presented to the audience), and decoding refers to how that information is interpreted by the audience. This approach places a strong emphasis on the role of language and symbols in constructing and conveying meaning, as well as the process of transforming pre-existing concepts into forms that can be understood and experienced by the audience.

The constructionist approach acknowledges that “neither things in themselves nor the individual users of language can fix meaning in language. Things don’t mean: we construct meaning, using representational systems- concepts and signs” (Hall, 1997, p.25). What consists of a representation system is the sounds we make, images, marks made on a canvas and digital impulses transmitted electronically (Hall,1997, p. 25).

As discussed in chapter one of Jenny Kidd’s book, ‘Representation’, “semiotics rests on an understanding that the words, languages and other signs which we use in our everyday and social context help to shape our reality” (2015). This is where the importance of representation in data lies. Before you can engage with data art without additional assistance in understanding the data, it is important to engage with traditional data visualizations, as data visualizations are now crucial to news media, papers, periodicals, on television and the internet. Such forms bring together images, symbols, tabular and graphical content and text in order to tell stories that have origins in data” (Kidd, 2015). Audiences are dependent on context before they can interact with data art. This context could be presented in a paragraph or it can be substantiated with a graph. ‘House of Cards’, by Aaron Koblin created with director James Frost is an excellent example of data art that would be in dire need of context before audiences can engage with it. ‘House of Cards’ is a three-dimensional data-driven music video that uses Lidar lasers and structured light scanners to scan the band Radiohead. Though this data art is expressed as a music video, there is no initial meaning produced to it, unless of course the person viewing the music video is a fan of Radiohead. It would have been preferred if the creators of this artwork explained how the data was captured to animate the band’s movements as well as the process of syncing the movements made by the artwork to the music, the meaning produced can be extended to people who observe data as well as to the general public. I believe that the suggestion would have influenced the audience to engage further with the artwork, aside from the artwork sparking curiosity.

In conclusion, the exploration of data representation through the lenses of data visualization and data art reveals a diverse array of strategies, purposes, and impacts. While data visualization serves as a valuable tool for simplifying complex information and aiding decision-making through visually appealing and easy-to-understand presentations, data art takes a different approach. It aims to captivate and engage its audience on an emotional and aesthetic level, pushing the boundaries of traditional representation.

However, the difference between data visualization and data art goes beyond their objectives. It also lies in their creative processes. Data visualization follows strict guidelines and principles to ensure clarity and simplicity, while data art embraces a more unconventional and artistic approach, using basic visual elements to convey deeper meanings in visually striking and thought-provoking ways.

The ideas presented in "Artistic Data Visualization: Beyond Visual Analytics" by Fernanda B. Viégas and Martin Wattenberg highlight the transformative power of data, blurring the lines between art and data representation. Additionally, Sabine Roeser's exploration of how art can spark ethical reflection and Stuart Hall's perspective on representation as a construction further emphasize the significant roles that both data art and data visualization play in shaping our understanding of the world and promoting critical thinking.
